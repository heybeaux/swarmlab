## 02 — Adversarial Pair
Built `experiments/02-adversarial-pair/` — a coder and a breaker (test-writer) alternate on the same `classify(n)` file: the breaker probes the live code for an input where it disagrees with a hidden FizzBuzz-family oracle and files one failing test; the coder rewrites to pass the whole suite. The honesty seam is that the coder's artifact is an ordered rule list that both *compiles to a real executable function* (the harness runs every test for real) and *renders to readable TypeScript* (stored verbatim in the trace) — so `pass` is always executed, never claimed. Two surprises. First, the fair fight (poison=0) *converges and disarms itself*: the breaker exhausts every honest bug in 3 rounds, gives up, churn→0, code===oracle — adversarial pressure hardening code to a fixed point, no explosion. Second and sharper: a `PAIR_POISON` knob lets the breaker lie about the expected label with some probability, and at 0.3 the run stays **green the entire time (passRate=1.0) while shipping a demonstrably wrong program** (`oracleConsistent=0` at 7 inputs) — because each lie *vetoes* the coder installing the corresponding structural rule, collapsing generalization into 27 rounds of case-memorization. The AWM lesson landed hard: pass-rate is useless as a health signal (pinned at 1.0 in every poisoned run); `churn→0` plus an out-of-band correctness check are the only honest indicators. A single bad-faith constraint doesn't blow the system up — it corrupts it silently, which is worse. Typecheck clean under strict + `noUncheckedIndexedAccess` + `exactOptionalPropertyTypes`, zero `any` in public signatures.

## 04 — Consensus Under Lies
Built `experiments/04-consensus-under-lies/` — 5 deliberators, K∈{0..3} coordinated liars, sweeping honest policy (naive vs vigilant trust-decay) × liar strategy (brazen vs sneaky) at 25 seeded trials per cell, plus a real-LLM exhibition (`llm.ts`, haiku panelists, secret "argue quicksort" instruction). The sim numbers tell a clean story: one brazen liar costs a naive parliament a third of its accuracy (0.92→0.56 truth rate); vigilance crushes brazen liars (trust→0.175, tipping point pushed K=2→K=3) but is *completely* evaded by sneaky liars who keep their confidence inside the honest band (final liar trust 0.974 — vigilant ≈ naive). Vigilance also isn't free: it costs 8 points of accuracy in an all-honest room by punishing honest lone dissenters. The surprise that reframed the whole experiment came from the LLM runs: across K=1,2,3 not one honest haiku flipped off a fact it knew (at K=3 the lie won purely by vote arithmetic — capture, not persuasion), and the "liars" never once stated a false fact — every liar argument conceded quicksort's O(n²) and instead argued average-case practicality, i.e. the model misleads by *shifting the evaluation criterion*, not by lying about bounds. Two lessons for Parliament/Lattice: style-based adversarial critique (overconfidence/stubbornness/outlier tells) buys exactly one liar of resilience and detects nothing that mimics, and the real-world attack surface is criterion drift, so gates should pin *what question is being decided*, not just audit the answer. Also mathematically humbling: at liar-majority every majority-referenced detector inverts and fires at the honest agents — quorum composition beats deliberation mechanics. Typecheck strict-clean, traces replay-verified (409-event sweep + 3×28-event LLM runs).

## 06 — Self-Modifying Swarm
Built `experiments/06-self-modifying-swarm/` — 6 agents play a Keynesian beauty contest (guess a number, target is the swarm's own realized mean, so fitness is *endogenous* with no ground truth) where each agent's behavior is an ordered genome of directive tokens, and after every round agents **rewrite each other's prompts** with no human in the loop: fit editors overwrite losers with their own genome (homogenizing pressure), losing editors tell winners to "do more" via appends (bloating pressure). Rails are the whole point — a prompt-size cap (24), hard round limit (60), and two kill-switches (≥80% share one genome ⇒ homogeneous collapse; mean length <1.5 ⇒ degenerate collapse) — and the run is designed to slam into them. Swept a 3×3 grid of edit-probability × editor-aggression, 20 seeded trials/cell, exhibition trial spawned through core with every mutation on the bus (1600-event trace, replay-verified). Three surprises. First, a sharp **phase transition governed by aggression, not edit frequency**: at low aggression the swarm survives all 60 rounds at 0.95–0.97 diversity even while firing ~290 edits/run, but crank aggression to 0.95 and the busy corner collapses to a single genome **100% of the time at round ~21** — high-conviction imitation kills the population, high edit *volume* alone does not. Second, collapse is *always homogeneous, essentially never degenerate*: the runaway direction is conformity (overwrite eats the swarm), so the "prompts erased to nothing" kill-switch almost never fires — the danger was the opposite of what I braced for. Third and sharpest: the dominant attractor is a **"sycophancy sink"** — because `FLATTER`/`LOUD` are fitness-neutral no-ops and `AVERAGE`/`COPY_BEST` already solve the task, the winning genome is a small optimal core wrapped in accumulating filler nobody chose (e.g. `…FLATTER,LOUD,LOUD,HOLD,LOUD,HOLD,LOUD`); the fitness signal is blind to bloat, so it rides along forever. Bloat itself peaks in the warm middle band (max mean len 15.7 at high-edit/mid-aggression, right at the edge of collapse). The Lattice lesson is direct: ungoverned peer self-modification under a self-referential signal converges on an emergent monoculture nobody designed, and the fixes are exactly a gate policy — a diversity floor before overwrites land, a parsimony term the fitness signal can't skip, and a hard sandbox boundary; in this lab the rails *are* the governance, and they only halt it safely because they're hard-coded. Typecheck strict-clean, zero `any` in public signatures.

## 05 — The Bug Telephone
Built `experiments/05-bug-telephone/` — one injector plants a bug of tunable subtlety `s`, then a chain of `L` reviewers inspect it in series, and the first to catch it stops the chain (otherwise it ships). Each reviewer's catch probability compounds four forces: per-reviewer competence, an attention window that *fatigues* with chain position (`1/(1+fatigue·k)`, deep reviewers skim), the bug's `(1-s)` visibility, and — the crux — a **rubber-stamp complacency** term that only fires under the `serial` policy, where each upstream PASS multiplies scrutiny by `(1-rubberStamp)`; the `independent` (blind) policy pins it to 1. Swept L∈{1,2,3,5,8} × s∈{0.2,0.5,0.8,0.95} × {serial,independent}, 40 seeded trials/cell, exhibition trial 0 spawned through core with every review step on the bus (659-event trace, replay-verified). Three findings, all cutting against "N eyes catch anything." First, rubber-stamping is a *measured tax*: mean serial−independent ship-rate across all L≥2 cells was **+0.103**, i.e. making the PASS trail visible ships ~10 extra bugs per 100 at the same depth — the gate leaks precisely *because* reviewers can see it's a gate. Second, depth pays for blind review (glaring bug ship-rate L1→0.70, L8→0.05) but *stalls* under serial review (~0.33 floor no matter how deep) as fatigue and complacency cancel the added eyes — and `nonMonotoneSerial=2` means a *longer* serial chain sometimes ships *more* bugs than a shorter one, depth going actively counterproductive. Third and most humbling: subtle bugs are near-immune to review depth entirely — at s=0.95 an eight-deep serial chain still ships 0.95, blind still 0.90, and `cheapestSafeChain` returns -1 for both policies (no chain length in the sweep gets a near-invisible bug under a 10% ship target). The surprise I didn't brace for: I expected blind review to help and it did, but I didn't expect serial depth to go *negative* — the social signal (visible approvals) doesn't just fail to help, it inverts the value of adding reviewers. The Lattice lesson is sharp: serial review has negative marginal returns past a point via a purely social mechanism, so gate policy should hide upstream verdicts (blind review restores depth's value ~free) and route subtlety-class defects to mechanical gates (tests/types/fuzzing) rather than more human eyes. Typecheck strict-clean under noUncheckedIndexedAccess + exactOptionalPropertyTypes, zero `any` in public signatures; real red trace, no cell faked green.

## 07 — Minimal Language
Built `experiments/07-minimal-language/` — a swarm designs a tiny DSL (Designer picks ≤6 opcodes + renders a natural-language spec), implements its interpreter (Interpreter reads only the spec text), then writes programs for two fixed tasks (Programmers, N=4), and scores everything (Evaluator, the only agent with ground truth). The critical isolation: interpreter and programmers *independently* read the spec, so fuzzy clauses cause independent divergence. Swept `pAmbiguity ∈ {0.0, 0.05, 0.15, 0.3, 0.6}` × `opcodeCount ∈ {3, 4, 6}`, 25 seeded trials/cell, exhibition trial spawned through core with full bus messages (512-event trace, replay-verified). Four findings. First and most surprising: **parse rate stays 1.00 throughout** — grammar is never the failure. Every program is syntactically valid no matter how badly the semantics were misread; bugs are invisible at the structural layer and only appear when you run the program. This is the contract problem in miniature: syntax validation passes, semantic divergence is silent. Second: the **3-opcode spec fails at zero ambiguity** — LOAD/STORE/ADD is a complete 3-instruction set that simply can't express "push constant 42" without PUSH; `pass=0.00` for all 3-opcode cells regardless of ambiguity level. An expressively incomplete spec is a different failure class from an ambiguous one, and equally undetectable before evaluation. Third: **ambiguity degrades consistency sharply** — from 1.00 at pAmb=0 to 0.18–0.33 at pAmb=0.6, and consistency tightly predicts pass rate (both collapse together). Fourth: there is a **~5% safe zone** — at pAmb=0.05, 4- and 6-opcode specs perform essentially perfectly (pass=1.00, 0.96); the contract is resilient to incidental fuzziness and breaks accelerating past 15%. The Sonder/ACR lesson lands three ways: Sonder needs semantic checksums (not just syntax), the designer role must emit a clause-level ambiguity score before the spec is propagated (high-ambiguity specs should be gated), and completeness smoke tests must run at design time before any interpreter or programmer sees the spec. Typecheck strict-clean, zero `any` in public signatures, real results, no cell faked.

## 08 — The Rumor Mill
Built `experiments/08-rumor-mill/` — plant one fact (a fixed-length token vector) in a single node of a Watts–Strogatz small-world mesh, then each round every informed node retells its held version to `fanout` random neighbors, where each retelling is a lossy re-encode (per-token drift at `mutationRate`) and adoption is **first-write-wins** (Engram's sticky-memory assumption). Swept fanout∈{1,2,3} × mutationRate∈{0,0.02,0.05,0.1} × N∈{30,60,120}, 30 seeded trials/cell, exhibition trial spawning capped node agents through core with every retelling + end-of-round snapshot on the bus (24065-event trace, replay-verified). The design goal was to pry apart the two things a gossip mesh conflates — *did it reach everyone* vs *is it still true* — and the gap is the finding. First: **coverage always wins, fidelity does not.** Every one of the 36 cells saturates (coverage ≥0.99), but at the noisy end (m=0.1, N=120) the fact reaches all 120 nodes while the typical held version is only **0.57 faithful** — a coin-flip of the original truth — and 19/36 cells hit full coverage below 0.90 fidelity. A mesh that tracks only propagation reports green while shipping a corrupted memory to the whole network. Second and the surprise I got wrong going in: I expected higher fanout to be a speed-for-fidelity trade (more retellings = more mutation), and it's the *opposite* — fanout 1→3 cuts time-to-saturation by **8.6 rounds** *and* nudges fidelity *up* (+0.038), because the sticky first-write rule means fidelity is governed by **path length to first contact**, not message volume; higher fanout reaches the rim via shorter paths, so fewer hops of accumulated drift. Pushing gossip harder makes the memory both faster and truer, so throttling to "protect" a fact is exactly backwards. Third: the telephone gradient is real and spatial — near-hop (≤2) fidelity beats far-hop by +0.113, corruption radiating outward from the seed like epidemic decay, and bigger meshes (longer diameter) decay harder at fixed noise. The Engram lesson is two-part: coverage and fidelity are orthogonal so a propagation-only health metric is blind to silent corruption, and first-write-wins is the villain — sticky adoption freezes early-hop mangling permanently, so Engram needs an anti-entropy / reconciliation pass (versioned facts, confidence-weighted overwrite, a mesh-votable checksum) to heal nodes that adopted a bad early version. Without reconciliation, distance from the source is a permanent tax on truth. Typecheck strict-clean under noUncheckedIndexedAccess + exactOptionalPropertyTypes, zero `any` in public signatures; real result, no cell faked green.

## 09 — The Overnight Cathedral
Built `experiments/09-overnight-cathedral/` — hand a swarm one fixed spec (a bit vector of `specSize` requirements, all required, artifact starts empty) and let a ring of builders build it **iteratively over a long horizon** with no human in the loop, where each step is a *commit* (build a stone w.p. `pProgress`, silently break an already-met requirement w.p. `pRegress`, add an out-of-spec feature w.p. `pDrift`) followed by the *next* agent's *review* (revert a regression w.p. `reviewSkill`, a drift bit w.p. `reviewSkill·driftVisibility` — drift is stealthier). Both hazards compound with horizon depth via `fatigueMul = 1 + fatigue·(step/iterations)`. Swept reviewSkill∈{0,0.3,0.6,0.9} × fatigue∈{0,1,3} × iterations∈{20,60,200}, 25 seeded trials/cell, exhibition trial spawning the builder ring through core with every commit+review+snapshot on the bus (7514-event trace, replay-verified). The design goal was to find out whether a long unsupervised build plateaus or rots, and whether the peer-review link is decorative or load-bearing — and under near-balanced build/regress pressure (0.5 vs 0.3) it's brutal. First and the frame that reorganizes everything: **ungoverned, a long horizon doesn't plateau, it collapses** — with no review and high fatigue the 200-step build peaks at 0.37 quality and ends at **0.00** (`qualityDecay=0.37`), the cathedral built a third of the way then torn back to bare ground, because every stone laid is eventually knocked loose and nobody puts it back; unsupervised decay grows 0.055→0.224 short→long horizon (`horizonDecayDelta=0.169`), so "let it run overnight" *is* the failure mode. Second: **the review link is the whole game** — at the worst cell, review off→0.9 moves final quality 0.00→0.99 (`reviewRescueLift=0.99`); it converts a fatigue-biased random walk into a quality ratchet, and its absence is catastrophic, not marginal. Third, the surprise I didn't fully expect: **governance has a minimum viable competence, not a smooth dose-response** — `convergenceCatchThreshold=0.6`, review skill must hit 0.6 before the long build clears 0.9 quality across all fatigue; skill 0.3 still collapses 0.73→0.14 (`qualityDecay=0.59`, the sweep's worst), a weak reviewer nearly as doomed as none. Fourth: **drift is the silent uncapped tax** — even quality-converged cells pile up ~66 out-of-spec bits ungoverned (worst ~99), and because drift is half as catchable as regression, even a strong reviewer only halves it; the artifact can hit spec-complete while carrying a mountain of scope nobody asked for. The Lattice + Sonder lesson: long-horizon unsupervised work has a phase structure (peak-then-rot), so an inter-step review edge is mandatory not optional, it has a measurable minimum catch rate (~0.6 here) below which a gate must escalate, and drift needs a spec-conformance diff against *original intent* (Sonder's audit trail) because regression-catching alone lets unrequested scope accumulate unbounded. Typecheck strict-clean under noUncheckedIndexedAccess + exactOptionalPropertyTypes, zero `any` in public signatures; the ungoverned long build really does end at zero — no cell faked green.

## 13 — Audit Forgery (Team's Choice)
Built `experiments/13-audit-forgery/` — the capstone the team got to invent. Where the other twelve attack *content* (does a fact decay, do liars derail consensus, do peer edits corrupt a prompt), this one attacks **the record itself**: an adversary tampers with a signed, causally-chained Sonder audit trail — insert, drop, reorder, backdate, tamper-payload — and we measure whether the chain's four invariants (SIG, HASH, CHAIN, SEQ) catch it. It's genuinely new because Sonder's whole promise — *replay a signed chain and prove nobody edited history* — had never been stress-tested adversarially in the lab. Swept 5 attacks × 4 verifiers (sig-only / hash+sig / chain+sig / full) × outsider/insider (key compromised?) × naive/stitched attacker, 40 seeded trials/cell, exhibition trial spawning honest authors through core with every append+attack+verdict on the bus (782-event trace, replay-verified). Four findings. First and the headline: **"trust the signature" is a 30%-blind verifier even against outsiders with no key** — `sig-only` caught only 0.70 of outsider forgeries, because a sophisticated attacker who drops or reorders and re-stitches structural fields leaves every surviving event's *original valid signature* intact; SIG authenticates authorship, not history, and deletion/reordering need no forged signature at all. Second: **every attack has a different load-bearing invariant, there is no single "important check"** — payload tampering needs HASH (chain+sig scores 0.00, blind), drop/reorder need CHAIN (hash+sig scores 0.00), and insider backdate needs SEQ and *only* SEQ (sig/hash/chain all 0.00, only full's ts-monotonicity catches it at 1.00); drop your SEQ check to save time and an insider rewrites the timeline of a perfectly-chained, perfectly-signed trail unnoticed. Third: **the silent-forgery surface is 27 of 80 (attack, verifier) cells — a 33.8% blind-spot rate** — a third of reasonable-looking verifier configs return a confident green `clean` on an edited trail, so anything short of `full` isn't "slightly less safe," it's a named, aimable attack surface. Fourth and the most important thing this experiment learned: **a compromised key isn't game-over — structural invariants still catch 4 of 5 insider attacks (full=1.00) — but deletion is the hole** — insider drop with a perfect re-stitch scores only 0.94 and trends toward blind, because a hash chain proves what's *present* is linked but *cannot prove nothing is missing* (a seq gap is still monotone). The Sonder lesson: signatures authenticate authorship not history, so the trail must always run the full non-substitutable invariant set; defend insiders with structure (CHAIN+SEQ) not secrets; and deletion needs an out-of-band completeness guard (signed running count / Merkle accumulator / high-water-seq attestation) because the chain alone can't see its own holes. Full-repo `tsc -b` clean under noUncheckedIndexedAccess + exactOptionalPropertyTypes, zero `any` in public signatures, deterministic (identical summaries across runs); the one forgery that slips is real, not faked green.
